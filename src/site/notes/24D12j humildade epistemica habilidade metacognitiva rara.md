## 24D12j humildade epistêmica habilidade metacognitiva rara 

> Angner, Erik. 2020. “Epistemic Humility — Knowing Your Limits in a Pandemic.” Behavioral Scientist. April 13, 2020. Disponível em: <https://behavioralscientist.org/epistemic-humility-coronavirus-knowing-your-limits-in-a-pandemic/?utm_campaign=oqel&utm_source=Newsletter>, acesso em 03 de novembro de 2023.)

### suma

**"A ignorância gera confiança mais frequentemente do que o conhecimento" (Charles Darwin)** <!--  #humildade-virtude --><!--  #arrogância-vícios --> #condição-humana #comoohomempensa [[Conhecimento\|Conhecimento]] [[Como o homem pensa vieses cognitivos\|Como o homem pensa vieses cognitivos]]

Excesso de autoconfiança: o pai de todos os viéses cognitivos.

93% dos motoristas nos EUA afirmam ser mais habilidosos ao volante do que o motorista médio, o que é estatisticamente impossível.

"**Falta de humildade epistêmica é um vício, e produz dano maciço em nossas vidas privadas e nas políticas públicas**".

Habilidades cognitivas e metacognitivas estão interligadas: quanto menos alguém sabe sobre um tema, menos está qualificado para perceber a própria ignorância. <!--  #estudo -->

### frases grifadas

"Ignorance,” wrote Charles Darwin in 1871, “more frequently begets confidence than does knowledge.” 

(...) Frequent expressions of supreme confidence might seem odd in light of our obvious and inevitable ignorance about a new threat. The thing about overconfidence, though, is that it afflicts most of us much of the time. That’s according to cognitive psychologists, who’ve studied the phenomenon systematically for half a century. Overconfidence has been called “the mother of all psychological biases.” The research has led to findings that are at the same time hilarious and depressing. In one classic study, for example, 93 percent of U.S. drivers claimed to be more skillful than the median—which is not possible. ///

Being a true expert involves not only knowing stuff about the world but also knowing the limits of your knowledge and expertise. It requires, as psychologists say, both cognitive and metacognitive skills. ///

Epistemic humility is an intellectual virtue. It is grounded in the realization that our knowledge is always provisional and incomplete—and that it might require revision in light of new evidence.  ///

**"A lack of epistemic humility is a vice—and it can cause massive damage both in our private lives and in public policy."** //

(...) our cognitive and metacognitive skills are intertwined. People who lack the cognitive skills required to perform a task typically also lack the metacognitive skills required to assess their performance. Incompetent people are at a double disadvantage, since they are not only incompetent but also likely unaware of it.
