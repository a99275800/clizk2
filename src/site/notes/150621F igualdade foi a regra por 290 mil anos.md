## 150621F igualdade foi a regra por 290 mil anos

> STERELNY, Kim. How equality slipped away. Aeon. **For 97% of human history, equality was the norm**. What happened?

Our particular species of humans has been around for about 300,000 years and, best as we can tell, **for about 290,000 of those years we lived materially poorer but much more equal lives **(...) In contrast with subsistence farmers, foragers are indulgent towards their children (...) **every community contains aggressive, ambitious individuals who’d like to be leaders. Foragers keep these upstarts on a short leash**.

how did inequality establish and grow without the cloak of law and the shield of organised state power? (...) 
the critical problem is to **explain inequality in village societies where it doesn’t yet have the protection of institutionalised power**.

One such scaffold to inequality was **the emergence of clan structure**. Clans have a **strong corporate identity, built around real or mythical genealogical connection**, reinforced by demanding **initiation rites **and intense collective activities.

The second development was the **emergence of a quasi-elite based on the control of information, which created a hierarchy of prestige and esteem**, rather than wealth and power. This was originally based on **subsistence skills**.
this **economy of information **can **include story and music**, and the same can be true of its ritual and normative life
oblique social transmission (...) puts **manipulation **on the agenda (...) the **centralised transmission of the norms, rituals and ideology **of a community can easily favour one group at the expense of others (...) **gerontocracy**, with the **power of older men residing in their control of esoteric knowledge**

These scaffolds became potent as communities gave up movement in favour of a **settled life **(...) **Farming and storage **make inequality possible, perhaps even likely, because they **tend to undermine sharing norms, establish property rights **and the coercion of labour, amplify intercommunal violence, and lead to increases in social scale

**For mobile foragers, sharing is insurance. Hunting especially is very chancy** (...) the social rewards of generosity are important, and the social costs of refusing are high since the intimacy of forager camps makes success hard to conceal
**Storing**, like sharing, **is a way of managing risk**, and farmers are more likely to store than to share (...) **Variation in supply within the community is likely to flow from variation in commitment and effort, not differences in luck**
Local bad luck – unfavourable weather, a plague of pests – will probably affect everyone in a community, which makes sharing a poor form of insurance

**Crop farming is also arduous and time-consuming** (...)  It would simply be a **bad idea **for people to **commit to these efforts without something like property rights** (...) it would be maladaptive for children to remain with their parents working their land unless these rights **included a right of transfer**. 

So storage, especially **storage **based on crops, will tend to **produce a community of independent family economies** (...) With **inheritance**, that will in itself tend to **produce wealth inequalities**, if only because family size variation will force some families to divide their pie more finely than others

Storage opens the door to **coerced labour**. (...) Labour on farms is **spatially focused **and often much **less skilled**. It **can be supervised **and controlled. Moreover, it’s tempting to do so, as much farm work is intrinsically unappealing (...) 

Storage also makes intercommunity violence more likely. (...) 

**farming tends to lead to population growth**. (...) An increase in **community size matters**, for many of the social **mechanisms that keep alphas in check **in forager communities are scale-dependent. They **depend on intimacy and trust**. 

**storage **will often **lead to a surplus**, (...) that surplus is available as **a tool in local politics** (...) can be used to **build prestige by transmuting it into high-status goods or services**

(socialismo, comunismo, igualdade, heranças, propriedade privada, sociedades afluentes, revolução agrícola)
